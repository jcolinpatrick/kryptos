## Le Carré Endings (pre-1990) — The Pattern

Every le Carré ending available to Sanborn shares a structure: **someone arrives at a place, and the arrival reveals the cost of everything that came before.**

- *The Spy Who Came in from the Cold* (1963): Leamas reaches the top of the Wall. His lover is shot below. He climbs back down to die with her. **The rescue was the trap.**
- *Smiley's People* (1979): Karla walks across the Glienicke Bridge from East to West. Smiley has won. He feels nothing. **The victory is empty.**
- *A Perfect Spy* (1986): Pym finishes his confession and shoots himself. **The truth was the weapon.**
- *The Russia House* (1989): Barley walks into Moscow to be with the woman he loves. **Love defeats loyalty.**

Common pattern: **arrival + revelation + moral weight**, compressed into a final image.

Le Carré ending qualities:
- **Pyrrhic** — victory costs everything
- **Quiet** — no explosions, just someone walking, stopping, or falling
- **Morally ambiguous** — unclear who won or if winning matters
- **Geographically precise** — specific places (the Wall, a bridge, a room)
- **Brief** — the final moment is a single image or action

## What "He Wrote Several and Chose One" Tells Us

This is a **fiction writer's process**. You don't write multiple endings for a technical message. Multiple endings means:
- The ending **reframes** everything that came before
- Each option changed the **meaning** of the whole work
- The ending is the **point** of the sculpture
- The preceding text could support multiple conclusions — the ending is **authored**, not inevitable

### What Sanborn would reject:
- Anything **too satisfying** — decades of effort shouldn't feel simply rewarded. "I tricked them. Nobody has it all."
- Anything **too cryptic** — effort shouldn't feel wasted. He cares about the solver's experience.

### What Sanborn would keep:
- Something that makes the solver **sit quietly for a long time** — not celebrating, not frustrated, but thinking
- The le Carré ending: you close the book and stare at the wall

## The Genre Shift

K1-K3 is Howard Carter. K4 is le Carré. The pivot from archaeology to espionage IS the ending's meaning: the sculpture's real subject was never ancient tombs. The archaeology was metaphor. The real subject is intelligence — what happens when you uncover something that was meant to stay hidden. You open the tomb, and inside you find not gold but a compass bearing and a clock in a Cold War city.

Sanborn put this at CIA headquarters. The ending is for the people who work there. It says something about the nature of their work — that the secrets they keep and the secrets they uncover are the same kind of thing, and knowing them changes nothing and everything.

That's le Carré's central insight, and it's the ending Sanborn chose.
