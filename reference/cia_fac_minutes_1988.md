## Meeting 1: Special FAC Meeting — 29 November 1988

### Purpose
Review Jim Sanborn's concrete proposal for the New Headquarters Building (NHB) sculpture, and review five graphics artists for a separate NHB contract.

### Attendees
- Marilyn Farley, GSA Art and Architecture Group
- Four NEA (National Endowment for the Arts) panel members
- James Sanborn
- CIA personnel (names redacted 25X1): AC chairman, AC members, interior designer (OL), adviser to FAC, Deputy Director for Administration, Executive Director CIA, Executive Assistant DDA

### Sanborn's Proposal — Key Details

**Three installation sites** (not just the courtyard):
1. **Walkway** from West Lot parking structure to the new building
2. **Northwest corner of the courtyard** between the two buildings — with a **small whirlpool**
3. **Eastern edge of the courtyard** against the cafeteria glass wall — with a **larger, still pool** surrounded by **miscanthus grass** and a **meteorite from the southwestern United States**

**Materials**:
- **Red granite** (primary) — slabs
- **Verdigris copper plates** "pierced by frequency code alphabet tables"
- **Lodestone** (secondary material)
- **Petrified wood** (secondary material)

**Timeline**: Estimated 1 to 2½ years to complete. (Actual: exactly 2 years, Nov 1988 → Nov 1990.)

**Flexibility**: Sanborn "noted that he was still refining the details of the project, and asked to be allowed some flexibility in that refining process."

### Reception
"All members of the NEA panel, as well as all Agency personnel listed above, were enthusiastic about the proposed theme of the sculpture and Sanborn's early ideas for its execution."

### Suggestions
- Two slabs (not three) for the walkway site
- Style/placement of walkway lights and courtyard picnic tables might need redesign once sculpture was in place

### Graphics Artist Candidates (for separate NHB contract, replacing Matt Mulligan)
1. **Charles Luce** — signage and symbols specialist, unidimensional mode, intimate scale
2. **Terry Allen** — performance/installation artist, light/sound enhancements, Vietnam War text references
3. **Tom Green** — large-scale paintings resembling Australian aboriginal paintings
4. **Jonathan Burafski** — flat presentations of world flag symbols
5. **Vernon Fisher** — relies heavily on narrative text

CIA preference: Charles Luce and Tom Green. Panel to telephone each artist to determine willingness to work for the Agency.

## Analysis — What Changed from Proposal to Final Build

### Cross-reference: Smithsonian Oral History + 1989 CIA Letter

The oral history and 1989 letter confirm the three-site design was built, with some evolution:

**Site 1 (Walkway/Entrance) — BUILT AS PROPOSED**:
- Red granite strata (3 pieces: larger on left, two on right as you enter)
- Morse code copper plate between granite layers ("like an ore, or a page in a book")
- Lodestone on top of copper plate on right side
- Sandblasted compass dial with needle "deflected metaphorically from true north" by lodestone

**Site 2 (NW Courtyard, Main Ensemble) — BUILT WITH EVOLUTION**:
- Petrified tree: "a strange surrogate, a memorial really, for the lost trees" (employees' forest was cut on a weekend when no one was there)
- S-curved copper screen supported by petrified tree (cipher + Vigenère tableau, ~2000 letters)
- Pool at base of screen with WHIRLPOOL water: "under stress, spinning around like a whirlpool"
- Green quartz + red slate on plaza as benches/barriers ("red and green, stop or go, danger or safety")
- Note: FAC proposed whirlpool at Site 2 — CONFIRMED in oral history

**Site 3 (E Courtyard, Cafeteria Wall) — BUILT BUT SIMPLIFIED**:
- Two red granite strata segments separated by calm pool with aquatic plants
- **NO encoded text**: "These two strata's, and pool, exist without the stress of encoded text"
- Contemplative space. Ornamental grasses.
- Placed visible from BOTH covert and overt sides of divided cafeteria
- **METEORITE: NOT MENTIONED** in oral history or 1989 letter. Dropped during "refining."

### Elements confirmed built:
- Red granite slabs at all three sites
- Verdigris copper plates with cipher/tableau ("frequency code alphabet tables")
- Both pools: turbulent whirlpool (Site 2) AND calm still pool (Site 3)
- Three-site distribution (walkway, NW courtyard ensemble, E courtyard strata+pool)
- Lodestone with compass dial (Site 1)
- Petrified tree (Site 2)
- Ornamental grasses (Sites 2 and 3)
- Green quartz and red slate (Site 2)

### Elements that CHANGED from FAC proposal:
- **Meteorite DROPPED**: Proposed for Site 3 in FAC minutes, absent from oral history and 1989 letter. Either dropped during refinement or concept absorbed elsewhere.
- **Petrified wood → Petrified TREE**: FAC says "petrified wood" as secondary material. Oral history reveals it's a full petrified tree that structurally supports the copper screen. More prominent role than "secondary material."
- **Miscanthus → ornamental grasses**: Generic ornamental grasses confirmed, specific miscanthus species may have changed.

### Observations for K4 Research

1. **"Frequency code alphabet tables"** — Official CIA description of what became the Vigenère tableau + ciphertext. The term "frequency code" suggests Sanborn may have pitched the sculpture in frequency analysis terms.

2. **The meteorite — DROPPED** — Proposed for Site 3 but vanished from all later descriptions. An extraterrestrial object from the southwestern US. Either concept was abandoned or absorbed into another element. Worth tracking: did any meteorite-related material end up in the installation?

3. **Lodestone** — A natural compass material. "EASTNORTHEAST" is a compass bearing. A lodestone literally points a direction. Sanborn says: lodestone deflects compass needle "metaphorically from true north." Placed at ENTRANCE (Site 1), not the cipher screen.

4. **Petrified tree** — Not just "petrified wood" as secondary material (FAC), but a FULL petrified tree that structurally supports the copper screen. "A strange surrogate, a memorial really, for the lost trees." Organic matter transformed to stone over geological time — a literal PALIMPSEST of time.

5. **Water as deliberate dual symbol** (from 1989 letter):
   - Turbulent/whirlpool at cipher screen = "provocative, constantly agitated into standing waves"
   - Calm/still at eastern strata = "reflective, contemplative"
   - Binary opposition: encoded/stressed vs unencoded/peaceful

6. **The unencoded Site 3** — Sanborn deliberately made the cafeteria site have NO encoded text. In a sculpture whose entire purpose is encoded text, this absence is itself a statement. Visible from both covert and overt sides of the divided cafeteria.

7. **Covert/overt cafeteria** — The CIA cafeteria was divided with a wall: covert employees on one side, overt on the other. Wall ran to the courtyard window. Both sides saw the calm, unencoded strata. This physical division between secret-keepers and non-secret-keepers mirrors the sculpture's themes.

8. **Diagonal geological axis** — Entrance outcrops and courtyard outcrops on same N-S diagonal axis. Sanborn: "I didn't line it up with anything particular to the Agency. I wanted to make it natural as if it had always been there." The strata cut across the CIA's organizational structure like a pre-existing geological formation.

9. **Progressive complexity** — Confirmed by Sanborn: Morse (entrance, simple) → Vigenère (courtyard, complex) → K4 (courtyard, hardest). "Increases in complexity as you move through the piece."

10. **The lost forest** — Employees loved the forested slope visible from cafeteria. "Things got so heated that the trees had to be cut at night on a weekend." The petrified tree is a memorial to this loss. Sanborn's sensitivity to the site's history.

11. **Webster's personal approval** — DCI + DDCI briefed and approved Dec 19, 1988. Not just a facilities project.

12. **Sanborn consulted on security barriers** — His artistic authority extended beyond the sculpture to surrounding infrastructure design.

### Meeting 1 (29 November 1988)

A special meeting of the Fine Arts Commission took place 29 November 1988 to review the concrete proposal offered by James Sanborn for a sculpture for the new building and to review slides of five graphics artists' work in support of a decision on a contract that would substitute for that earlier offered Matt Mulligan. The meeting was attended by Marilyn Farley of GSA's Art and Architecture Group, by four members of the National Endowment for the Arts panel that she has put together for the Agency compound project, by James Sanborn, and the following Agency Personnel: [names redacted 25X1] AC chairman, AC member, AC Member, AC member, interior designer OL adviser to FAC, Deputy Director for Administration, Executive Director CIA, Executive Assistant DDA.

The model presented by Sanborn showed a sculpture composed primarily of slabs of red granite, with verdigris copper plates (pierced by frequency code alphabet tables), lodestone, and petrified wood used as secondary materials. Sanborn proposed locating elements of the sculpture at three sites: the walkway from the parking structure in West Lot to the new building, the northwest corner of the courtyard formed by the two buildings, and the eastern edge of the courtyard against the glass wall of the cafeterias. His maquette showed the inclusion of a small whirlpool at the second site and of a larger, still pool surrounded by miscanthus grass at the third. The third site would, moreover, include a meteorite to be obtained from the southwestern United States. He estimated that the project would take 1 to 2½ years to complete, noted that he was still refining the details of the project, and asked to be allowed some flexibility in that refining process.

All members of the NEA panel, as well as all Agency personnel listed above, were enthusiastic about the proposed theme of the sculpture and Sanborn's early ideas for its execution. A few suggestions were made: that two slabs, vice a possible three, be used for the walkway site, and that the style and placement of walkway lights and courtyard picnic tables might need to be redesigned once the sculpture was in place.

The five artists considered for the graphics contract for the new building were: Charles Luce, a specialist in the use of signage and symbols, whose work tends to be unidimensional in mode and intimate in scale. Terry Allen, a performance artist who specializes in installations that often have light and sound enhancements and text that refers, on many occasions, to the Vietnam War. Tom Green, a producer of large-scale paintings that (in the words of this recording officer) bear a strong resemblance to Australian aboriginal paintings. Jonathan Burafski, who specializes in flat presentations of world flag symbols. Vernon Fisher, who relies heavily on narrative text in his pieces.

The NEA panel had [redacted] in their rankings of the suitability of the artists. [Redacted] speaking for the Agency, expressed a preference for giving top consideration to Charles Luce and Tom Green. Because questions were raised by NEA panel members regarding the willingness of several of these artists to take on a contract for the Agency, it was agreed that [redacted] would telephone each of the five, determine that willingness, and then --by a telephonic conference call-- get the sense of the panel. The panel's recommendations will then be forwarded to Chairman, FAC.
