KRYPTOS: From The Source
1.The Story
The fascinating story of the Kryptos artwork at the Central Intelligence
Agency headquarters has been unfolding for almost 20 years. From the
top down struggle at the Agency about the highly publicized work to its
robust life on the Internet, its involvement with the Da Vinci Code and its
powerful effect on individuals and popular culture, the artwork has taken
on a fast-paced almost mythic life.
In the late 1 980's when the piece was designed codes and coding were
relatively esoteric subjects. Code had not really penetrated the popular
mindset. The well-publicized arrival of Kryptos (it was front page news on
a global scale and was featured on national and international radio and
television broadcasts) set the stage for a code revival. The blossoming of
personal secrecy and code cracking as recreation have been gaining in
popularity ever since. And the fact that an artist, with a little help,
developed a challenging code enhanced its unconventional populism.
Kryptos had deftly steered itself onto a parallel track with the advent of
bank machine pass codes, personal pass codes, Internet secrecy, and
even, as you will see in the following text, national security.
The K4 section of Kryptos is widely thought of as one of the world's most
famous unsolved mysteries. Recently it was the most Googled thing
globally for two days running. Kryptos websites, chat rooms, and biogs
abound.
We are fast approaching the 20th anniversary of the works installation so
the timing is right and, in addition, the author is going to imbed significant
clues to K4 in the text of this book.-The book itself would be a mix of
text, code graphics and photographs. The text (with a forward by the
noted tech writer Steven Levy) will be written by Jim Sanborn the artist
of Kryptos and will consist of lively first hand accounts of the intrigue of
Kryptos. And detailed descriptions and graphics of the historic and
contemporary encoding systems used on Kryptos. The designers of the
enclosed Wired Magazine profile of Kryptos have agreed to execute the
elaborate interactive code graphics. Other images will include the original
coding charts used by Sanborn, other encoded works by Sanborn and lots
of " insider" crypto material.

2. The Background
For the last 30 years I have been making art inspired by science. From
Archaeology, to cryptography, to physics, my artistic continuum has
waxed and waned scientific. At times the allusions to science are subtle at
times obvious, at times the distinction between science and art is blurred
and at times some viewers of my work fail to find art in my science. Wide
interpretations are fine with me and further the discussion.
In the late 1 970's I began working with geological forces, those slow but
gigantic processes that formed the Earth we know. My gallery and
museum installations included geological cutaways of stone strata's that
had been eroded in some way. In 1978 I started including lodestones, a
naturally magnetic ore of iron. I collected large numbers of these from the
Iron mining areas of southwestern Utah. Some of these weighed tons and
some were fist sized. I pulled the large highly magnetized ones from the
soon to be destroyed mountaintops using mile long cable attached to a
bulldozer. I shipped these evocative rocks with the magical magnetic
properties back to my studio an included them in both my installations
and the public art works. One was destined for a future CIA project.
*The lodestones on exposed mountaintops are magnetized by repeated
lightning strikes over a period of thousands of years. In the historic past
the residents of Cedar City Utah who would gather to watch the lightning
show during storms have corroborated this visual treat.
For millennia lodestones had been considered magical. The ancient
Chinese used lodestone spoons in a primitive con game, they were spun
by geomancers AKA shamans who knew they would always come to rest
in the same orientation and thus they could move the symbols beneath
the spoon to falsely predict the "future" of their unwitting Emperor. The
Greeks believed that if you sailed a ship too close to a lodestone
mountain the nails in the ships hull would be pulled out and the ship would
fall to the bottom of the sea. Tribal chiefs in the mountains of Africa had
large, powerful lodestone gates to their villages as the first metal
detectors, to locate the hidden iron weapons of their visitors.
I liked the invisible forces of lodestones. I liked the invisible forces of the
Earth's magnetic field. And I liked the invisible forces of the Coriolis

2a. The Site At CIA Central
There are really two main entrances to the CIA complex of buildings.
There is the Eastern entrance built in the 1940's, the one made familiar in
movies and television, where the great seal of the Agency is inlayed into
the floor and which is flanked by the two long "star" walls listing the fallen
covert and overt employees. This eastern entrance is for cleared visitors
and VIP's.
The Western entrance is the main employee entrance and it was new in
1 990. Hundreds of employees walk up a wide walkway from the parking
lots and enter the complex thru these doors.
Between these two main entrances, between the old and new buildings is
a large enclosed verdant courtyard with large trees and lawn. This central
courtyard is accessible from the north and south through doors in the
two glass hallways connecting the old and new buildings on either side of
the central courtyard.
Facing the central courtyard on the old building side is the large employee
cafeteria. For many years and into the early 1990's the cafeteria was
divided in half with covert employees on one side and overt on the other.
The separating wall ran to the window to the courtyard so both sides
could have a view.
So this was the setting I selected on the architectural plans for the
location of the several elements of Kryptos. It would require something
complex to have sufficient impact on so large a site.
I decided to have both the form and the content of the piece develop as
the employees entering the building traveled from the parking lots
through the entrance, through or into the new building and to or around
the central courtyard.

2c. The Courtyard Ensemble
The Kryptos elements in the central courtyard had to be far more
complex in both form and content. I decided to use another red granite
stone outcrop similar in form to the one outside the entrance and to
place it on the same axis as well, so seen from an airplane the two are
parallel, as they should be in a stratified geological sense.
This second set of outcrops consists of two segments of strata separated
by a pool of calm water with aquatic plants. The agency could add fish if
they desired. The idea was that this was a contemplative space. These
two strata's, and pool, exist without the stress of encoded text. The pool
and strata's were also placed so that they could be seen by both the
covert, and the overt, sides of the cafeteria.
Before the new headquarters building was built the view from the split
cafeteria was of a beautifully forested slope. The employees were very
fond of the view and protested vigorously in its defense, but the plans
were fixed, and the forest had to go. Things got so heated that the trees
had to be cut at night on a weekend when no one was there. It was a
tough loss for many.

4. Despite The Rogue Elements
In the spring of 1990 we were building the courtyard elements of
Kryptos. It was a lot like building a ship in a bottle because anything we
placed in the courtyard had to be brought through the standard size glass
doors in the glass hallway on the west side of the courtyard. The thick,
heavy slabs of granite and the petrified tree were the most troublesome.
The slabs were quarrfed in South Dakota and were shipped to the large
employee parking lot on the north side of the Agency complex. The
tractor-trailers passed through the security gate, were caref.ully
scrutinized, deposited their loads, and left the same way. The lot
perimeter was basically the Agency perimeter and consisted of a high
chain link fence with motion sensors and cameras and it was patrolled
regularly.
The trucks from South Dakota left their trailers so I could unload the slabs
with a rented crane, as I needed them. I was drilling, splitting, and
finishing the ten to twelve thousand pound slabs on the trailers in the
parking lot. I had finished the granite outcropping outside the entrance
first and was in the midst of building the courtyard outcrop when one of
the trailers loaded with these shaped slabs went missing. We had worked
on the slabs the previous Friday but when we went to the lot on Monday
the trailer was not there. I raced over to the security gate and no one
remembered the trailer leaving. In fact, the stone and the trailer were
never found, it had vanished without a trace.
Surprisingly, the Agency quickly agreed to reimburse m~ for the lost
stone. I am not sure how the trailer loss was resolved. Within a few weeks
the stone was reordered, delivered, and reshaped in the same lot, without
incident. It was rumored at the time that the disappearance was an
attempt to sabotage Kryptos by a certain high-level rogue operative
within the Agency. The operational lack of finesse didn't seem quite up to
CIA standards though, and why bother?

3. Cutting The Code
Revere Copper Products, the little shop that was started by Paul Revere in
the 1760's, made the copper plates we were cutting in the studio.
Copper is a base metal, an element on the periodic chart; one has a
certain reverence for such materials. The challenge was how to cut
almost two thousand, two inch tall letters of code through 3/8"inch thick
copper plate with sufficient precision to call it art.
We could cut each letter by hand with jigsaws (that would be almost as
brutal as chiseling a petrified tree out of solid rock). Or, the letters could
be cut by a robot using a relatively new process at the time called
waterjet, (50,000psi water pressure with the a little garnet dust to give it
a tooth). The problem was that with very few shops doing the process
there was no competition and the estimates were $250,000 for the letter
cutting alone, the entire CIA commission price. Today the same job costs
just $20,000.
We reluctantly began cutting the letters by hand, since I hadn't written
the plain text yet we started cutting the Verginieries Tableaux plates first.
With two assistants cutting five days a week it took eight months, twenty
assistants nine jigsaws and nine hundred blades. I had helpers that started
having nightmares about letter cutting, they didn't last long.
The cutting began by spraying the copper sheets with black paint and
scribing horizontal straight lines for the rows of letters through the dry
paint. We then checked and double checked the proper location of each
letter in the Tableaux and placed a metal stencil for each on the long
straight lines and traced each letter with a scribe. What was left by this
operation were thin, bright copper colored outlines of the coded letters.
Each letter was then drilled through several times so the saw blade could
be inserted to cut out the copper inside the scribed lines. After the
letters were cut each one was carefully filed so the edges of the cutting
precisely followed the scribe lines. This cutting routine; checking, scribing,
checking again, cutting and filing produced from six to ten finished letters
per day per person, and there could be no mistakes.
After eight months it became clear that very few people could handle the
stress of cutting. When we started on the encoded plates K 1 - K4 only
two assistants remained, after two months only one, very focused guy

6. Clandestine Collaboration
The only way to describe my mathematical ability is with a word of my
own; I am an anathamath, one who is confounded by mathematics. In high
school I never had a summer vacation, I was always in summer school for
math and even during the school year I had a tutor. So .... .l had a problem
or as luck would have it perhaps an asset. Despite the common belief that
cryptography is a mathematical science I believed that there must be a
way to do an end run around that science and, if I was very lucky, score.
Despite my fondness for the spy novel and Le Carre in particular,
literature did not give me the necessary encoding tools. I needed some
help, so I went through the possibilities: Total emersion in mathematics,
specifically, algorithms, Not!. .... getting a working knowledge of Navaho,
right!... .. The Brit's ... MIS or Ml6, the Russians, Mousad etc. I went around
and around with ways of using cryptographers from the outside. But
realized they would hardly take it seriously and they couldn't be trusted,
could they? The other problem was General Services Administrations
public art contracts have a "buy American" clause that stipulated that I
had to use US expertise.
I was aware of various historic coding systems like Vignieres Tableaux etc.
but it was coming up on the 21 st Century I needed contemporary
expertise to stump contemporary and future code-breakers. So I turned
back, or inward depending on how you look at it. Who better to stump
the chumps than a former stumper? ..... Enter Edward Scheidt.
Ed had retired from the Agency to start his own crypto based security
company.

7. Persistent Enigma
From my first trips as a child to the western US in the 1950's my prized
possession was the rock hammer my dad bought me. For many summers
we looked for fossils from the Badlands of South Dakota to Wyoming, and
back east during the rest of the year I found fossil skeletons on the
shores of the Chesapeake Bay. In the tall grey cliffs on the Maryland and
Virginia shorelines I excavated the skeletons of Cetotheres Sixty five
million year old aquatic mammals that looked like a cross between a
porpoise and an alligator a "porpogater" I found a baby no more that a
foot long and a large adult that had an eight foot long scull. I donated the
specimens I collected to the Smithsonian Institution and to a local
University. I mostly enjoyed the act of discovery, searching for a
treasured object long and very hard and ultimately finding it. Two of my
fondest memories were at the Library of Congress one was seeing the
Dead Sea scrolls that my dad had put on display at the Library, their first
showing in the US. And the other was seeing my father as the Director Of
Exhibitions, handcuffed to the Guttenberg Bible while it was being
transported to New York for exhibition.
All of these exciting moments were owed to something kept from view
for a very long time and then seen. When I wrote the plain text for
Kryptos I wanted the mystery to unravel slowly, and I wanted it to be
discovered, but only after a lot of time and effort.
I worked on the plain text for a very long time, months. I had originally
thought to have a prominent fiction writer like John Le Carre AKA David
Cornwell contribute to the text and even sent a letter to his publisher but
I never got a reply. This was actually a good thing, because I realized fairly
quickly that the fewer the people that know the text the better. As a
child I had considered Howard Carters journal notes on the discovery of
King Tut's tomb to be one of the most exciting tales of discovery ever
told. It was up there with Heinrich Schliemann's discovery of Troy and the
nineteenth Century dinosaur discoveries in the western US. And Gobi
desert.

8. Spooks on Ladders
I wasn't paying much attention to the din created by the shift change in
the parking lot of the DC Transit Police headquarters, when one of the
officers said "hey we ran off a couple of suits, on ladders, looking into
your studio" I asked, 'did they have cameras?' "Yeah, and listening
devices .... friends of yours" I said 'no', and 'thanks'.
The building the parking lot surrounded was ideal; big, high-ceilings,
skylights, "secure". A former hay storage building for the 1 9th century
carriage trade, artists loved it. (I owed my ownership to underwear, I had
bought the building a decade earlier with a commission from Gordon
Haines the inventor of the seamless stocking and an old family friend.) Its
structure was perfect for making large work; its previous owner gave us
such things as the writhing figures on the 22' high tympanum frieze for
the National Cathedral and the three bronze figures for the Vietnam
Memorial.
I used the building for a very different kind of art. It was the summer of
1988 and my assistants were hard at work cutting out letters of encoded
text for Kryptos, my piece for CIA central, or (as those in the employ call
it,) "Langley".
It seemed a little ridiculous, really .... spies on ladders? But considering the
events of the previous year, it wasn't that weird. People were beginning
to talk about Kryptos. Presidential aspirant Patrick Buchanan called the
piece the as yet unseen piece "a pile of dinosaur feces", while the Village
Voice was trolling NY artists studios with the question " Hi, I'm from the
Village Voice, would you take a commission from the CIA? They didn't
get a lot of strikes on that one.
And then there were the cartoons.

5. The Department of Historical Intelligence
So somewhere on that beautifully minimal stretch of highway from
Tucumcari NM to that 72oz steak in Amarillo TX I devised a plan. While I
understood, in Agency jargon, their "need to know" the plain text of
Kryptos before it was put in their yard, it was not something I would give
up easily, if at all.
Institutionalized paranoia not withstanding I could have written porn,
scorn, or just have been overly forlorn for their taste. They really wanted
someone in their family to see it.
I thought, well. .. perhaps George H W Bush former DCI and sitting
President .... I just couldn't .... I was too much the Democrat, and he
probably couldn't be bothered. What about William Webster, current DCI,
smart fellow ... mmm.
The Agency hastily came up with The Department of Historical
Intelligence. This body consisted of three individuals: a male supervisor
and two female analysts they would be the curators/protectors of the
info to be forever interred.
This long drive from Holbrook Arizona to DC with a freshly disinterred
petrified tree in a rental truck was fertile ground for plotting to inform the
Historical Intel folks with the text without allowing them remember
it.
So ... I figured that if there are three people in the room I cut the full text
into single sentences, scramble them, and give each person a third of the
text. They read the pieces, get the gist of it, and I take back the pieces,
easy! But what if all three have photographic memories, get together, and
reconstruct it from memory ... highly unlikely, but possible, this is after all
the Central Intelligence Agency. Well, as it turned out I made a lucky
gamble that only one of them might have that ability, and made a second
set of scrambled sentences that was divided into only two parts.
June 1989, I enter a small windowless room approximately 1 O' wide by
12' long with a low ceiling. The supervisor was to my right with one
analyst seated near him. The other analyst was across the room, I posed a
trick question: 'I really want you to remember what I am going to show
you, do any of you have a photographic memory?' The supervisor and the

1 7. Beyond Kryptos
Many artists' lives can be divided into stylistic phases. My phases usually
last about 10 years, as I mentioned earlier in the 1970's geology was my
focus, the 1980's lodestones and invisible forces were the thing and in
the 1 990's the Kryptos project stimulated a wider interest in secret
subjects.
The Kryptos project was intense and fascinating all at once, but by 1994
the project was for me, old work. Bt 1998 I began focusing on a very
different aspect of secrecy. In between from 1995-1997 I took a break
and completed a series of large format projections in the desert in the
western US and in Ireland. I needed to clean the slate, and completed a
body of work totally unrelated to secrecy.
In the fall of 1 998 during my last projection trip I visited White Sands
National Monument In Southern New Mexico. This pristine and surreal spot
was just 20 miles south of the Trinity Site, the spot where the first
Atomic bomb was detonated on July 16, 1945. I grabbed some
pamphlets about the test and the implications of that test, and the
building of the first atomic bomb was my next stylistic phase. This phase
culminated with the exhibition and catalog titled Atomic Time exhibited at
the Corcoran museum in 2003 and the Gwangju Biennale in South Korea
in 2004.
With the advent of this nuclear phase I began testing the limits of the
secrecy establishment. My reconstruction of the atomic bomb laboratory
at Los Alamos included the, still classified as secret, interior "physics
package" of that first nuclear device. It was called Fat Man and in order to
construct it I needed precise measurements and the design of the device.
Since the device was still classified in the US that info was hard to find
but, there were still several sources of information. The design was not
classified in the former Soviet Union and there was an anti-nuclear
website run by Chuck Hansen that had the largest repository of Russian
nuclear documents. The rationale of the anti nuclear movement at the
time was that nuclear secrets were in part responsible for the arms race.
If everyone had the pertinent nuclear info then no one would fight over it,
remember, this was before 911 and the North Korean and Iranian nuclear
programs.
