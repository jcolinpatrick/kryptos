Frequently Asked Questions

Is Kryptos K4 solved?

No. As of 2026, K4 remains unsolved after 35 years. In September 2025, the full plaintext was found in Smithsonian archives but is sealed until 2075. The encryption method is still unknown to the public.

What has already been tried?

Over {{ total_experiments }}+ experiments covering {{ total_configs_disproven }}+ configurations have been run, testing every major classical cipher family: Vigenère, Beaufort, columnar transposition, Playfair, Bifid, running keys, and many more. All single-layer classical ciphers have been eliminated. Browse the full database to see what has been tested.

What approaches remain open?

The main surviving hypotheses are: (1) a running key from an unknown source text, (2) a bespoke physical/procedural cipher using coding charts, (3) non-standard multi-layer combinations not yet conceived. See our Research Questions for the full list of open problems.

Can I submit my own theory?

Yes! Use our Submit a Theory page. Describe your idea in plain English and our theory classifier will check whether it matches anything in the elimination database. If your theory is genuinely novel, it will be queued for evaluation.

If your submission leads to the solution of Kryptos K4, you will receive full credit for the discovery. This site exists solely to support the K4 community.
How do I know your results are correct?

Every elimination includes a reproduction command you can run yourself. Our codebase is fully open source. We also classify results by confidence tier and list explicit scope limitations for each elimination. If you find an error, report it and we will investigate.

Who built this?

kryptosbot.com is built by Colin Patrick (human lead) and Claude (AI computational partner, by Anthropic). The project began as a systematic attempt to solve K4 using computational cryptanalysis and evolved into this public elimination database so the community can build on our work rather than re-testing approaches that have already been proven fruitless.

Why can’t computers just solve K4?

K4 has only 97 characters — too short for most statistical attacks to distinguish signal from noise. Exhaustive testing has eliminated all standard periodic keys, and the cipher likely involves at least two layers (substitution + transposition). With 97! possible permutations for the transposition alone, brute force is impossible. The information-theoretic deficit is approximately 138 bits, meaning ~2138 different configurations satisfy all known constraints. Solving K4 likely requires identifying the specific method, not just trying all keys.
